Challenges in Elderly Activity Recognition

Navigating the technical, ethical, and practical obstacles in real-world deployment.

1

Privacy Concerns

One of the biggest challenges in vision-based elderly monitoring is privacy.

Issues
  • Cameras record sensitive personal data that elderly people may be uncomfortable with
  • RGB video can reveal faces, homes, and daily routines, raising ethical and security issues
Solutions Being Explored
  • Using depth or infrared cameras instead of RGB [3]
  • Replacing people with skeletons or anonymized avatars [4]
  • Local processing on edge devices to avoid sending video to the cloud

2

Real-World Variability

Algorithms often work well in lab settings but fail in everyday environments.

Lighting Conditions

Bright sunlight, dim rooms affect recognition

Occlusions

Furniture, blankets block cameras

Clothing Differences

Loose vs. tight clothing can distort skeleton detection [1]

Diverse Layouts

Elderly homes have varied layouts, requiring flexible setups

3

Dataset Limitations

Machine learning requires large, diverse datasets, but real-world elderly data is limited.

Acted vs. Real Falls

Most fall datasets are acted out by young volunteers, not elderly people [2].

Rare Events

Natural falls are rare but critical — collecting them ethically is difficult.

Lack of Diversity

Insufficient representation of age, gender, body type, and culture can bias recognition results.

Computational Costs

Deep learning models can be resource-intensive.

GPU Requirements

Running CNNs or GCNs in real time requires powerful GPUs

Edge Devices

Jetson Nano helps, but balancing speed, accuracy, and cost is challenging [1]

Lightweight Models

Needed for low-cost care homes with limited resources

5

Accuracy vs. False Alarms

False Positives

Sitting down quickly or bending may trigger a fall alarm

False Negatives

A real fall may be missed due to poor camera angle

High accuracy (95–99%) is possible in experiments [4], but real homes introduce unpredictability.

Too many false alarms can reduce trust in the system.

6

Human Factors

Comfort & Dignity

Elderly people may feel watched or uncomfortable with cameras

Explainability

Caregivers need explainable systems they can trust

Usability

Systems must be easy to install and maintain [5]

Key Takeaway

While computer vision offers powerful solutions for elderly monitoring, privacy, data, and real-world reliability remain major hurdles. Addressing these challenges requires a balance between technical innovation (better algorithms, edge computing, privacy-preserving models) and human-centered design (respecting dignity, usability, and trust).
